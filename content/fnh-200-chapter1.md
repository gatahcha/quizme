# FNH 200: Food Science and the Canadian Food System

## Overview

FNH 200 (Exploring Our Food) is an introductory course examining food science principles and the Canadian food system. The course, taught at UBC from September to December 2025, provides students with foundational knowledge about how food is produced, processed, distributed, and consumed in Canada. Students learn to describe key concepts of food science, articulate personal values regarding food choices, and critically evaluate food-related issues. The course covers the multidisciplinary nature of food science, including chemistry, physics, microbiology, and engineering applications. Through examination of the Canadian food industry's structure, processing methods (using apples as a case study), and consumption trends, students gain comprehensive understanding of the journey from farm to consumer.

The curriculum emphasizes practical applications of food science discoveries, from historical innovations like canning and freezing to modern trends in food consumption. Students explore how controlled atmosphere storage extends shelf life, how various processing methods create diverse products from single raw materials, and how consumer preferences shape the food industry. The course includes assignments, quizzes, exams, and participation components, with a focus on developing critical thinking skills about food production, safety, and nutrition.

### Food Science Fundamentals

**Summary**: Food science applies basic scientific and engineering principles to study the physical, chemical, and biochemical properties of food, while food technology applies this knowledge to ensure food quality and safety.

**Detailed Content**:

Food science represents the application of fundamental scientific disciplines to understand food at molecular and systems levels. It encompasses chemistry to understand food composition and reactions, physics to study properties like texture and heat transfer, microbiology to examine microorganisms' effects, and engineering to design processing systems. This multidisciplinary approach enables researchers to acquire new knowledge about food behavior during processing, storage, and consumption.

Food technology differs from food science as the practical application domain. While food science generates knowledge, food technology utilizes this information to develop appropriate technologies ensuring quality and safety in commercial food production. Technologists implement scientific discoveries in processing plants, quality control laboratories, and product development facilities. This distinction mirrors the relationship between basic and applied sciences in other fields.

The field differs importantly from both agriculture and nutrition. Agriculture focuses on primary production—growing crops and raising animals—using inputs like sunlight, water, fuel, fertilizer, and soil. Nutrition examines food effects on people who consume them, studying how nutrients affect human health and disease. Food science occupies the middle ground, transforming agricultural products into safe, nutritious, appealing foods ready for consumption.

Historical pioneers established food science as a distinct discipline. Nicolas Appert developed canning through heat processing in bottles in the early 1800s, winning Napoleon's prize for food preservation innovation. Clarence Birdseye revolutionized freezing in the 1920s after observing that fish frozen rapidly in Arctic conditions maintained better quality than conventionally frozen products. These discoveries exemplify how observation, experimentation, and application of scientific principles advance the field.

**Key Points**:
- Multidisciplinary field incorporating chemistry, physics, microbiology, engineering, and analysis
- Food science generates knowledge through research and experimentation
- Food technology applies scientific knowledge to practical production systems
- Distinct from agriculture (production) and nutrition (consumption effects)
- Historical discoveries continue influencing modern food processing

**Examples**:
- Food chemists analyze how proteins in egg whites create foam structure in meringues
- Food microbiologists identify beneficial bacteria for yogurt fermentation
- Food engineers design pasteurization systems that kill pathogens while preserving nutrients
- Food technologists optimize freezing rates to minimize ice crystal size and maintain texture

#### Historical Discoveries and Pioneers

**Summary**: Key historical figures made breakthrough discoveries in food preservation that established the foundation for modern food science and technology.

**Detailed Content**:

Nicolas Appert, a French confectioner, made the first major breakthrough in food preservation in the early 19th century. In response to Napoleon's 12,000 franc prize offer in 1795 for innovative food preservation methods, Appert developed a technique involving placing food in bottles, corking them, and heating the sealed bottles in a water bath. This process, later understood as thermal sterilization, successfully preserved foods for extended periods. Appert won the prize and published his methods in 1806, establishing the foundation for the canning industry. His discovery occurred decades before Louis Pasteur explained the scientific principles underlying heat processing's effectiveness against microorganisms.

Clarence Birdseye revolutionized frozen food technology in the early 1900s through careful observation during expeditions to Labrador for the U.S. Geographic Service. He noticed that fish meat exposed to Arctic air remained tender and fresh-tasting when cooked months later, unlike fish frozen using common methods of that era. Birdseye recognized that the secret lay in rapid freezing at extremely low temperatures, which created smaller ice crystals that caused less damage to cellular structures. He developed commercial quick-freezing equipment and processes, founding the frozen food industry. His surname became synonymous with frozen foods, and his company's distinctive packaging featuring his name continues to this day.

Additional innovations expanded food preservation options and convenience. Ultra-high temperature (UHT) processing combined with aseptic packaging (Tetra Pak) enabled shelf-stable milk and juices. High-temperature short-time (HTST) pasteurization improved efficiency while maintaining product quality. Freeze-drying technology, initially developed for space food programs, preserved foods while maintaining structure and nutrients. These discoveries demonstrate how systematic application of scientific principles solves practical food preservation challenges.

Modern innovations continue this tradition with instant noodles, instant pudding mixes, dehydrated foods for camping and emergency supplies, vacuum packaging to extend refrigerated shelf life, and spreadable margarines with modified fat crystals. Each innovation applies food science principles to meet consumer needs for convenience, safety, variety, and extended shelf life.

**Key Points**:
- Nicolas Appert invented canning through heat processing in early 1800s
- Appert's method predated understanding of microbiology but proved effective
- Clarence Birdseye discovered rapid freezing preserves quality better than slow freezing
- Birdseye's observations in Arctic conditions led to commercial quick-freezing
- Modern innovations continue applying food science principles to preservation

**Examples**:
- Cans of roast veal from 1824 and soup from 1856 demonstrate canning's longevity
- Birdseye quick-frozen fish maintains texture comparable to fresh fish after months of storage
- Freeze-dried ice cream developed for astronauts eating in weightless conditions
- UHT milk in aseptic Tetra Pak cartons remains safe without refrigeration for months

**Tags**: food-history, pioneers, preservation, canning, freezing, Nicholas-Appert, Clarence-Birdseye, innovation

### Canadian Food System

**Summary**: Canada's food system encompasses primary agricultural production, post-farm processing and distribution, and diverse market channels serving consumers, with significant economic impact and evolving consumption patterns.

**Detailed Content**:

The Canadian food system operates as an integrated network connecting primary production through processing to consumers. The system's economic magnitude is substantial, with Canadians consuming approximately 1,095 meals per year (3 per day), total annual food manufacturing product shipments reaching approximately $123 billion, and Canada ranking as the 10th largest exporter of value-added processed packaged food and seafood products globally, with exports totaling CAD $48.0 billion in 2022.

Primary agricultural production occurs across Canada's provinces, with regional specialization based on climate and geography. Animal production (beef, poultry, pork) and dairy milk production are distributed nationwide, with particular concentration in provinces with suitable grazing land and feed production. Cereal and grain production dominates prairie provinces. Tree fruits, small fruits, and berries (cranberries, raspberries, blueberries) concentrate in regions with appropriate growing conditions, particularly British Columbia and eastern provinces. Vegetable production occurs in various regions depending on crop requirements. Seafood comes from both Atlantic and Pacific coastal waters plus freshwater sources.

Post-farm gate processing applies food science principles to transform raw materials into consumer products. This phase includes grading, washing, cutting, cooking, freezing, canning, drying, fermenting, packaging, and other operations. Processing plants require human resources, energy, water, equipment, and packaging materials as inputs. The waste stream from processing often generates valuable co-products like animal feed, pectin extraction, or compost rather than disposal.

Distribution channels move products from processing facilities to consumers through various pathways. Fresh markets receive graded produce directly from farms or packing plants. Retail grocery stores stock both fresh and processed products from multiple suppliers. Food service operations including restaurants, cafeterias, and institutions purchase through specialized distribution systems. Direct farm markets connect producers with consumers, bypassing some intermediary steps.

**Key Points**:
- Canadian food manufacturing generates approximately $123 billion annually in product shipments
- Canada ranks 10th globally in processed food exports at $48 billion (2022)
- Primary production specializes regionally based on climate and geography
- Processing applies food science to transform raw materials into consumer products
- Distribution occurs through retail, food service, and direct market channels

**Examples**:
- Prairie provinces produce wheat and canola for domestic use and export
- British Columbia's Okanagan Valley grows tree fruits including apples, cherries, and peaches
- Atlantic provinces harvest lobster, crab, and fish for domestic and international markets
- Ontario produces vegetables for fresh consumption and processing into canned goods

### Apple Processing Case Study

**Summary**: Apple processing demonstrates how a single raw material transforms into diverse products through controlled atmosphere storage, thermal processing, fermentation, dehydration, and specialized preparations.

**Detailed Content**:

Apples serve as an ideal case study for understanding food processing because they transform into numerous products while demonstrating key preservation and processing principles. The journey begins with primary production on farms requiring inputs of sunlight, water, fuel, fertilizer, soil, and pest control. After harvest, apples move to packing plants for grading based on size, color, firmness, and absence of defects.

The grading process separates apples into different quality tiers. Highest quality apples with excellent appearance, proper size, and no defects go to fresh markets for direct sale to consumers. Medium quality apples may have minor cosmetic defects but retain good eating quality, often used for processing. Lower quality apples with more significant defects but still wholesome become ingredients for processed products or animal feed. Truly damaged or diseased apples go to disposal or composting.

Controlled atmosphere (CA) storage represents a critical technology for extending apple availability beyond harvest season. CA storage manipulates temperature, relative humidity, and atmospheric gas composition to slow respiration rate and delay ripening (senescence). For McIntosh apples, optimal storage conditions include temperature around 3°C, relative humidity at 87%, and atmosphere composition of 2.5% oxygen, 2.5% carbon dioxide, and 95% nitrogen. After six weeks, carbon dioxide increases to 4.5% while maintaining 2.5% oxygen and reducing nitrogen to 93%.

These CA conditions differ dramatically from normal atmospheric composition (21% oxygen, 0.03% carbon dioxide, 78% nitrogen). The reduced oxygen slows aerobic respiration and ethylene production, while elevated carbon dioxide further inhibits ripening processes. Nitrogen serves as an inert filler gas. Under optimal CA conditions, McIntosh apples store successfully for up to 8 months compared to only 5 months in regular cold storage at the same temperature and humidity. This extended storage enables year-round apple availability from autumn harvest.

Apples destined for processing follow multiple pathways. Juice production involves crushing, pressing, filtering, and typically pasteurization or commercial sterilization for extended shelf life. Some juice undergoes fermentation with yeast to produce hard cider containing alcohol and distinctive flavors. Cider can further ferment with acetic acid bacteria to create apple vinegar, a useful condiment and ingredient.

Apple sauce production involves cooking apples with added sugar (usually), resulting in a smooth or chunky product preserved through thermal processing in jars or cans. Pie fillings similarly involve cooking with sugar, thickeners, and spices, then canning for stability. These thermally processed products maintain quality for years at room temperature.

Dehydration creates several product forms. Sliced dried apples remove water to inhibit microbial growth and enzymatic reactions, creating shelf-stable snacks or ingredients. Fruit leather processes apple puree with added sugar into thin sheets that dry into pliable, concentrated products. These dried products serve as convenient snacks, breakfast cereal ingredients, and components in confectionery products.

Processing waste streams generate valuable co-products rather than true waste. Pomace (pressed apple solids) becomes animal feed or compost. Pectin extraction from apple cores and peels provides valuable gelling agent for jams and jellies. Even damaged apples unsuitable for direct processing often serve as animal feed rather than disposal. This comprehensive utilization improves processing economics and reduces environmental impact.

**Key Points**:
- Grading separates apples by quality for fresh market or processing use
- Controlled atmosphere storage extends shelf life from 5 to 8 months
- CA storage uses reduced oxygen (2.5%), elevated CO₂ (2.5-4.5%), and nitrogen
- Processing creates juice, cider, vinegar, sauce, pie fillings, and dried products
- Fermentation with yeast produces cider; bacteria ferment cider into vinegar
- Waste streams generate valuable co-products like pectin and animal feed

**Examples**:
- Fresh McIntosh apples in May originate from CA storage facilities, not current harvest
- Apple juice undergoes pasteurization at 85-90°C for 15-30 seconds to ensure safety
- Hard cider fermentation converts apple sugars to alcohol (typically 4-8% ABV)
- Fruit leather dries apple puree to approximately 20% moisture content for stability
- Pectin extracted from apple pomace gels jam and jelly at proper sugar/acid ratios

**Tags**: apple-processing, controlled-atmosphere-storage, CA-storage, fermentation, food-processing, preservation, case-study

#### Controlled Atmosphere Storage Technology

**Summary**: CA storage manipulates temperature, humidity, and atmospheric gases to slow apple respiration and ripening, extending storage life from 5 to 8 months for McIntosh variety.

**Detailed Content**:

Controlled atmosphere storage represents one of the most important post-harvest technologies for extending fruit availability beyond harvest season. The technology works by creating environmental conditions that slow but do not stop normal physiological processes in living fruit tissue. Understanding the science requires grasping how fruit respiration drives ripening and senescence.

Living apples continue respiring after harvest, consuming oxygen and producing carbon dioxide, water, and heat while breaking down stored compounds. This respiration process drives ripening—the development of desirable eating characteristics including softening, flavor development, and color changes. Eventually, respiration leads to senescence—over-ripening, excessive softening, off-flavors, and breakdown. CA storage slows this process by limiting oxygen availability (substrate for respiration) and increasing carbon dioxide (which inhibits certain enzymes).

The specific CA conditions vary by apple variety, as different cultivars have varying respiration rates, ethylene production, and sensitivity to low oxygen or high carbon dioxide. McIntosh apples, a popular variety in Canada, require specific conditions for optimal storage outcomes. Temperature around 3°C slows biochemical reactions without freezing (which would damage tissue). Relative humidity at 87% prevents water loss and shriveling while avoiding condensation that promotes decay.

The atmospheric composition creates the controlled atmosphere effect. Initial conditions of 2.5% oxygen (versus 21% in air) and 2.5% carbon dioxide (versus 0.03% in air) slow respiration significantly. Nitrogen makes up the remaining 95% as an inert filler. After six weeks, carbon dioxide increases to 4.5% while oxygen remains at 2.5% and nitrogen decreases to 93%. This adjustment reflects the accumulation of respiration-produced CO₂ and its additional ripening-inhibiting effects.

CA storage rooms must be airtight to maintain these controlled conditions. Monitoring systems continuously track oxygen, carbon dioxide, temperature, and humidity. Scrubbers remove excess carbon dioxide when levels exceed targets. Nitrogen generators or tanks supply nitrogen to replace oxygen consumed by respiration. The fruit itself consumes oxygen and produces carbon dioxide through respiration, requiring active management to maintain target levels.

The benefits of CA storage extend beyond simple time extension. Properly stored apples maintain firmness better than cold storage alone. Flavor compounds, both sugars and acids, retain better balance. Color remains more appealing with less yellowing or off-colors. Textural properties including crispness and juiciness persist longer. These quality benefits mean CA-stored apples better satisfy consumer expectations even months after harvest.

Different apple varieties require different CA conditions. Granny Smith apples, for example, tolerate lower oxygen levels than McIntosh. Red Delicious requires different temperature and humidity combinations. Honeycrisp demands particularly precise management due to sensitivity to both low oxygen and high carbon dioxide. Storage facilities often include multiple rooms with different conditions to accommodate various varieties.

CA storage applies beyond apples to many other fruits and some vegetables. Pears, kiwifruit, and avocados benefit from CA storage. Even some vegetables like cabbage and broccoli store better under modified atmospheres. The principles remain similar—slow respiration without causing damage from excessively low oxygen (which causes fermentation) or excessively high carbon dioxide (which can burn tissue).

**Key Points**:
- CA storage reduces oxygen from 21% to 2.5% to slow aerobic respiration
- CO₂ increases from 0.03% to 2.5-4.5% to inhibit ripening enzymes
- Temperature (3°C) and humidity (87%) work with gases for optimal results
- McIntosh storage extends from 5 months (cold storage) to 8 months (CA)
- Different apple varieties require customized CA conditions
- Technology maintains quality including firmness, flavor, and color

**Examples**:
- CA storage room atmosphere analyzer shows 2.5% O₂, 4.5% CO₂, 93% N₂ readings
- Fruit firmness testing shows CA-stored apples maintain 14-15 lbs force versus 10-11 lbs for cold storage
- Nitrogen generator continuously supplies N₂ to replace O₂ consumed by respiration
- CO₂ scrubber removes excess carbon dioxide when levels exceed 4.5% target

### Consumer Trends and Food Industry Response

**Summary**: Canadian food consumption patterns reflect demographic changes, health consciousness, convenience demands, and cultural diversity, driving food industry innovation and product development.

**Detailed Content**:

Understanding consumer trends enables food industry stakeholders to anticipate demand, develop appropriate products, and position offerings effectively. The ten major trends identified for the period approaching 2020 reflect fundamental demographic, social, economic, and cultural shifts affecting food choices.

Aging Canadians represents a critical demographic driver. As the population ages, demand increases for products addressing senior-specific concerns including bone health, heart health, cognitive function, and ease of preparation. Smaller portion sizes, easy-open packaging, and products addressing age-related health conditions gain importance. This trend influences everything from product formulation to package design to marketing messages.

Food for Health reflects growing consumer interest in using diet to prevent disease and promote wellness. Functional foods and beverages containing added nutrients, probiotics, omega-3 fatty acids, fiber, and bioactive compounds appeal to health-conscious consumers. This trend drives reformulation to reduce sodium, sugar, and trans fats while adding beneficial ingredients. Products highlighting specific health benefits, supported by scientific evidence and approved claims, continue gaining market share.

New Face of Canada acknowledges immigration's profound impact on food consumption patterns. Immigrant populations bring diverse food traditions, ingredient preferences, cooking techniques, and flavor profiles. This diversity creates demand for ethnic foods, specialty ingredients, and fusion products combining multiple culinary traditions. Supermarkets increasingly stock international ingredients, and restaurants offer diverse global cuisines. Food manufacturers develop products appealing to specific ethnic communities while introducing new flavors to mainstream consumers.

Veggie Movement encompasses multiple related trends toward plant-based eating, including vegetarianism, veganism, and flexitarianism (reducing but not eliminating animal products). Environmental concerns, animal welfare considerations, health benefits, and cultural factors all contribute. This trend drives development of plant-based meat alternatives, dairy alternatives, and products emphasizing vegetable content. Even consumers who continue eating animal products often increase vegetable consumption and occasionally substitute plant-based alternatives.

Small Indulgences reflects consumer desire for premium, indulgent products in moderate portions. Rather than eliminating treats entirely, consumers purchase higher-quality chocolate, premium ice cream, gourmet baked goods, and specialty snacks in smaller servings. This trend supports premiumization—consumers willing to pay more for superior quality, unique flavors, and artisan production methods. Single-serve portions satisfy indulgence desires while managing intake.

Changing Meal Patterns acknowledges that traditional breakfast-lunch-dinner structure increasingly gives way to grazing, snacking throughout the day, and meals at non-traditional times. Busy schedules, shift work, mobile lifestyles, and changing family structures all contribute. This pattern drives demand for portable foods, snack-sized portions, and products suitable for consumption anytime, anywhere. The distinction between breakfast foods, lunch foods, and dinner foods blurs as consumers eat according to preference rather than convention.

Evolving Society encompasses multiple social changes including more single-person households, dual-income families, urban densification, and changing gender roles. These shifts affect shopping patterns, cooking time availability, kitchen equipment ownership, and food preparation skills. Smaller household sizes mean smaller package sizes and more pre-portioned products. Time-pressed consumers seek solutions requiring minimal preparation time and cleanup.

Convenience remains paramount for busy consumers juggling work, family, leisure activities, and other demands. Ready-to-eat meals, meal kits, pre-cut vegetables, pre-marinated proteins, and simplified cooking instructions all address convenience needs. The definition of convenience evolves—once meaning fully prepared meals, now includes semi-prepared products requiring minimal finishing, providing some cooking satisfaction while saving time. Convenience extends beyond preparation to include shopping (online ordering), storage (pantry-stable products), and cleanup (single-use containers).

Educated Consumers increasingly research food products before purchasing, reading labels, checking nutrition information, investigating ingredient sources, and seeking information about production methods. Internet and smartphone access enables instant information verification while shopping. This trend drives demand for transparency—clear labeling, traceability, and communication about sourcing, processing, and nutrition. Companies respond with QR codes, websites, and detailed package information satisfying consumer curiosity.

Shifting Expenditures reflects household budget allocation changes driven by housing costs, transportation expenses, debt service, and competing priorities. While food remains essential, consumers increasingly seek value—maximum quality, nutrition, and satisfaction per dollar spent. This drives strategies including private label purchasing, bulk buying, price comparison, coupon use, and strategic splurging on items perceived as worth premium prices while economizing on others.

The 2023 trends update these concepts while adding new dimensions. Resizing the Restaurant/Retail Gap acknowledges how restaurants increasingly offer retail products (meal kits, packaged sauces, merchandise) while retailers expand fresh food offerings including in-store restaurants, food courts, and ready-to-eat meals. This convergence blurs traditional channel boundaries.

Morphing Meal Patterns expands the earlier trend observation, noting increased breakfast-for-dinner, lunch-style dinner meals, and complete rejection of meal timing conventions by some consumers. Product development must accommodate consumption at any time rather than assuming traditional meal occasions.

Sustainable Superstars reflects growing consumer emphasis on environmental impact, ethical production, and corporate responsibility. Products highlighting sustainability, carbon footprint reduction, responsible sourcing, and minimal packaging gain favor, particularly among younger consumers for whom these values significantly influence purchasing.

Everyday Self-Care positions food as part of wellness routines including stress management, energy optimization, and mood support rather than only disease prevention. Adaptogens, botanical ingredients, functional beverages, and products supporting mental wellness grow in popularity.

Gourmet Convenience combines the convenience and small indulgences trends, representing premium ready-to-eat or easy-to-prepare products delivering restaurant-quality results at home. These products enable consumers to serve impressive meals without extensive cooking skills or time investment.

- Ethnic diversity creates demand for varied cuisines and fusion products
- Convenience remains critical with evolving definitions beyond fully prepared meals
- Consumers increasingly research products and demand transparency
- Sustainability and environmental impact influence purchasing decisions
